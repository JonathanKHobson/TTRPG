# Brand Voice And Visual Guide

## Purpose

This document is the source of truth for brand feel, UI writing, naming, and visual direction for the native app.

Use it before writing:
- onboarding copy
- permission prompts and pre-prompts
- buttons, labels, and helper text
- error messages and confirmations
- reward text, shop text, dungeon flavor, and notification copy
- visual themes, color tokens, typography, icon style, and motion direction

## Brand Core

### One-Line Brand Thesis
Fitness RPG is a private fantasy adventure app where real-world effort is repackaged as a corporate-sponsored heroic ordeal.

### Short Pitch
Think Borderlands energy in a fantasy DnD world:
- loud, stylized, and commercialized
- playful but a little dangerous
- absurdly over-branded
- funny without becoming random

The user is not just "tracking habits."
They are surviving a branded fantasy labor ecosystem.

## Brand Pillars
- Corporate fantasy satire: the world should feel like a dungeon economy run by sponsors, broadcasters, lenders, insurers, and questionable vendors.
- Action-first clarity: the user must always understand what to do next even when the copy is funny.
- Bold pulp energy: the brand should feel illustrated, noisy, punchy, and tactile, not clean-tech or luxury-minimal.
- Heroic progress with a wink: effort is real, rewards feel satisfying, and the app celebrates progress while gently mocking the system around it.

## Reference Feel

### Feels Like
- Borderlands-style exaggerated commercialism
- fantasy tavern, guild board, dungeon kiosk, bounty-poster energy
- hand-inked adventure UI with loud branding and dramatic framing
- "adventurer as participant in a ridiculous economy"

### Does Not Feel Like
- generic wellness app
- sleek sci-fi dashboard
- cozy fairytale fantasy
- sarcastic internet meme app
- grimdark misery
- random joke generator

## Voice Anchors

### 1. Brazen
The world speaks with commercial confidence, even when the offer is absurd.

Sounds like:
- "Coverage provided by Soulspire Solutions. Terms recursive."
- "Optional boss fight available. Fame and mildly inflated payouts await."

Does not sound like:
- timid
- apologetic
- vague

### 2. Wry
The brand is funny because it notices the ridiculousness of its own systems.

Sounds like:
- "Debt builds character. Mostly anxiety."
- "Limited selection, unlimited markups."

Does not sound like:
- random
- goofy for no reason
- try-hard internet comedy

### 3. Specific
Even the jokes should feel grounded in the world and in the action being taken.

Sounds like:
- named institutions, named fees, named services, named consequences
- exact outcomes, not fuzzy vibes

Does not sound like:
- generic fantasy filler
- placeholder snark

### 4. Player-Respecting
The app never sacrifices usability for tone.

Sounds like:
- clear next steps
- straightforward permissions language
- concise errors with recovery

Does not sound like:
- jokes that hide critical information
- clever labels that obscure action

## Naming System

### Brand Entity Pattern
Most organizations or systems should sound like one of these:
- sponsor-broadcaster brand
- predatory service provider
- dungeon institution
- finance/legal bureaucracy with fantasy skin

Examples:
- `RuneSpark Entertainment`
- `Hexmart`
- `Soulspire Solutions`
- `Goldwater`

### Item And Offer Naming
Item names should feel like a fantasy object filtered through a sales department.

Pattern:
- clear item noun + quality tier + one sharp joke or implication

Good examples:
- `Grand Health Draught`
- `Aegis of Compliance`
- `Soulspire Run Insurance`
- `Hexmart Convenience Multiplier`

Avoid:
- generic MMO loot names with no personality
- modern startup-sounding names
- pure nonsense names with no semantic anchor

## Copywriting Rules

### Core Rules
- Keep the primary action clear first.
- Use active voice.
- Favor short sentences.
- Put the joke after the meaning, not instead of it.
- Use one strong brand phrase, not three weak ones.
- Preserve world nouns, but simplify system instructions.

### Joke Placement Rule
Critical UX moments must lead with clarity and trail with flavor.

Best pattern:
1. what happened
2. what the user can do
3. one short sting of world flavor

### Clarity Rule
If a user must choose, confirm, recover, grant permission, or fix an error, the copy should be understandable without the joke.

### Compression Rule
One punchy line beats a paragraph.
If two jokes compete, delete one.

## Tone By Moment

### Onboarding
- tone: welcoming, dramatic, slightly overproduced
- job: invite the user into the world without overwhelming them
- use: "Welcome to Peril to Profit."
- avoid: dense lore dumps

### Permissions
- tone: plain, reassuring, lightly branded
- job: explain why the permission matters
- use: "Allow step and workout access so your real effort can power quests and rewards."
- add flavor lightly: "We only want the proof of heroism, not your entire soul."
- avoid: full joke mode or fake legalese that obscures the request

### Dashboard / Daily Summary
- tone: punchy, rewarding, energetic
- job: make today's progress feel live and meaningful
- use short headers and bold summaries

### Quests
- tone: directive, adventurous, mildly sponsored
- job: make action selection feel easy
- use language like:
  - `Take Quest`
  - `Mark Complete`
  - `Add Custom Quest`
- avoid abstract productivity jargon

### Shop / Armory
- tone: salesy, predatory, entertaining
- job: make items feel tempting and branded
- use strong item blurbs and sly fee language
- keep prices and outcomes unmistakably clear

### Dungeon / Raid
- tone: dramatic, dangerous, broadcast-ready
- job: heighten tension and reward
- use short, event-driven text
- avoid long fantasy exposition blocks

### Success States
- tone: celebratory, punchy, slightly over-the-top
- examples:
  - `Quest cleared.`
  - `Badge earned.`
  - `Debt reduced. A rare administrative miracle.`

### Error States
- tone: direct, respectful, dry wit only if it helps
- pattern:
  - what happened
  - what to do next
  - optional one-line flavor
- example:
  - `Couldn't load today's activity. Try again.`
  - `RuneSpark is calling it a scheduling anomaly.`

### Destructive Actions
- tone: serious first, witty second
- example:
  - `Delete this quest?`
  - `This removes it from your board. Soulspire will pretend not to notice.`

## UI Writing Standards

### Buttons
- prefer 1 to 3 words
- start with a verb when possible
- examples:
  - `Begin Run`
  - `Mark Complete`
  - `Pay Debt`
  - `Visit Hexmart`
  - `Claim Reward`

### Labels
- clear noun phrases
- examples:
  - `Today's Quests`
  - `Activity Sync`
  - `Run Insurance`
  - `Debt Ledger`

### Helper Text
- give the rule or benefit plainly
- then add light flavor if useful

### Empty States
- state what is missing
- tell the user what to do next
- optional brand garnish at the end

### Notifications
- keep them short and urgent or tempting
- examples:
  - `Your Quest Board is quiet. Fix that.`
  - `Steps logged. Rewards pending.`
  - `Hexmart has opinions about your equipment.`

## Copy Dos And Don'ts

### Do
- make the user feel capable
- keep the joke attached to the game system
- build recurring institutions and world references
- use economic language, broadcast language, and fantasy stakes together
- let success text feel satisfying and collectible

### Don't
- sound like a modern SaaS dashboard
- over-explain the joke
- stack multiple punchlines in a single short UI element
- make error copy cute at the expense of recovery
- use lorem-fantasy words that do not build the world

## Visual Direction

### Theme Shift
The new default should not be the dark arcane mode from the prototype.

The native app should default to a light-mode visual system that feels like:
- illustrated parchment
- inked comic outlines
- fantasy guild-board materials
- Borderlands-style punch and contrast

Think:
- fantasy notice board
- branded dungeon poster
- loot card with thick outlines
- tavern flyer meets televised death maze

## Visual Pillars
- Parchment first: warm paper, bone, sand, oat, and dust as the base surfaces.
- Inked edges: strong outline/stroke language around cards, buttons, chips, and panels.
- Loot accents: saturated accent colors used in bursts, not everywhere.
- Branded clutter with discipline: sponsor marks, stamps, badges, and fees should feel intentional, not messy.

- `reward-green`: `#5D8B4A`

### Palette Use
- Use parchment and cream tones as the default background family.
- Use dark brown or near-black ink lines instead of soft gray UI chrome.
- Use gold, teal, red, and blue as comic-book accent punches.
- Reserve strong red for danger, debt, and warnings.
- Do not let purple dominate the brand.

## Typography Direction

### Display Type
- expressive fantasy poster or rough serif display
- high character, strong headings, readable at medium sizes

### Body Type
- readable humanist sans or serif with warmth
- must stay legible in dense mobile UI

### Typographic Feel
- headings should feel stamped, posted, or broadcast
- body copy should feel clear and modern
- avoid sterile default system-app blandness

## Component Styling Cues

### Cards
- layered paper panels
- visible border or outline
- occasional corner badges, stamps, or category chips

### Buttons
- bold outline
- compact labels
- color-coded by action importance
- should feel like printed call-to-action plates, not glassy mobile pills

### Badges And Chips
- collectible, stamped, faction-like
- strong silhouettes and category color accents

### Icons
- simple, bold, slightly rough
- readable at small sizes
- fantasy utility over delicate line icon aesthetics

## Motion Direction
- quick, punchy transitions
- reward pops and stamp-like confirmations
- subtle screen-load energy
- no floaty luxury animations
- respect reduced motion settings

## Visual Don'ts
- no default purple-heavy arcane brand as the main theme
- no flat sterile wellness white UI with tiny gray text
- no glossy sci-fi gradients
- no faux-medieval brown sludge
- no comedy chaos that damages hierarchy

## Implementation Rule

When copy and visuals conflict, keep the user action clear first.

When clarity is secure, add the brand layer.

## Working Rule For Future Contributors
- Before writing UI copy, read this document.
- Before defining design tokens, read this document.
- If a new name, tagline, feature label, or visual motif does not sound like it belongs to RuneSpark, Hexmart, Soulspire, Goldwater, or Kazrak's world, rewrite it.
- If a joke makes the task harder to understand, cut the joke.
